## 📚 Core Rule

German nouns are masculine (der), feminine (die) or neuter (das). The gender is intrinsic; there is no logical link to meaning, but many nouns follow predictable endings.

| Gender | Typical article | Example (masc.) | Example (fem.) | Example (neut.) |
|--------|----------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Masculine (der) | **der** | **der** Mann (man) | — | — |
| Feminine (die) | **die** | — | **die** Frau (woman) | — |
| Neuter (das) | **das** | — | — | **das** Kind (child) |

### General patterns for gender determination

- **Masculine (der)**: nouns ending in **-er**, **-en**, **-ig**, **-ling**, many profession titles, most days, months, seasons, and nouns with a male biological reference.
- **Feminine (die)**: nouns ending in **-e**, **-heit**, **-keit**, **-ung**, **-ion**, **-ur**, **-schaft**, **-tät** (e.g., *die Freiheit, die Zeitung*).
- **Neuter (das)**: nouns ending in **-chen**, **-lein**, **-ment**, **-um**, **-ma**, **-tel**. Also many infinitives used as nouns (*das Essen*).
- **Exceptions**: *das Mädchen* (girl) is neuter because of the diminutive *-chen*; *der Junge* (boy) is masculine.

> 💡 Remember: patterns are helpful, but many nouns must be memorised. When in doubt, consult a dictionary.

### Sample sentences

- **der** Hund — the dog (masc.) ✅
- **die** Katze — the cat (fem.) ✅
- **das** Buch — the book (neut.) ✅
- **die** Lehrerin — the female teacher (fem.) ⚠️ (note the suffix **-in** marks femininity).
