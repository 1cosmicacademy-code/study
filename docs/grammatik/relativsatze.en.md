# Relative Clauses (Relativsätze) in German

🎯 **Relativsätze** (relative clauses) are sentences within sentences — we use them to give **extra information** about a person or thing without starting a new sentence. Instead of "I have a friend. This friend lives in Berlin," we say "I have a friend **who lives in Berlin**."

## 💡 Relative Pronouns (Relativpronomen)

In German, relative pronouns are almost the same as definite articles: **der, die, das** — with small differences in Dativ and Genitiv.

| Case | Masculine (m) | Feminine (f) | Neuter (n) | Plural (pl) |
|------|---------------|--------------|------------|-------------|
| **Nominativ** | **der** | **die** | **das** | **die** |
| **Akkusativ** | **den** | **die** | **das** | **die** |
| **Dativ** | **dem** | **der** | **dem** | **denen** |
| **Genitiv** | **dessen** | **deren** | **dessen** | **deren** |

> 📌 **Note:** The relative pronoun takes the **same Gender** as the noun it refers to, but its **Case** depends on its role in the relative clause.

---

## ⚠️ How to choose the correct relative pronoun?

### Two simple steps:

1️⃣ **Gender of the noun (m/f/n/pl)** ← determines the row of the pronoun
2️⃣ **Role of the noun in the relative clause** ← determines the case (Nominativ / Akkusativ / Dativ)

### Example:

> **Der Mann**, **der** hier wohnt, ist Arzt.
> (The man who lives here is a doctor.)

- Noun: **Der Mann** ← masculine (m)
- Role of "der Mann" in the relative clause: he lives → Nominativ
- So: **der** (masculine + Nominativ)

> **Der Mann**, **den** du siehst, ist mein Bruder.
> (The man whom you see is my brother.)

- Noun: **Der Mann** ← masculine (m)
- Role: "der Mann" is seen by you → Akkusativ (you see **the man**)
- So: **den** (masculine + Akkusativ)

## 🎯 Word Order in Relative Clauses

📌 **Important rule:** In the relative clause, **the verb goes to the end**!

```
[Main clause], [relative pronoun + ... + verb]
```

**Examples:**
- Das ist der Film, **den ich gesehen habe**. — This is the film that I saw.
- Kennst du die Frau, **die dort sitzt**? — Do you know the woman sitting there?
- Das ist der Hund, **der im Garten spielt**. — This is the dog playing in the garden.

## ✅ Example Sentences for Each Case

| Case | Example | Translation |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Nominativ (m)** | Der Mann, **der** dort steht, ist mein Vater. | The man standing there is my father. |
| **Nominativ (f)** | Die Frau, **die** hier wohnt, ist Lehrerin. | The woman who lives here is a teacher. |
| **Nominativ (n)** | Das Kind, **das** schreit, ist müde. | The child that is crying is tired. |
| **Akkusativ (m)** | Der Hund, **den** du siehst, ist süß. | The dog that you see is cute. |
| **Akkusativ (f)** | Die Blume, **die** ich kaufe, ist schön. | The flower that I buy is beautiful. |
| **Dativ (m)** | Der Mann, **dem** ich helfe, ist alt. | The man whom I help is old. |
| **Dativ (pl)** | Die Freunde, **denen** ich schreibe, wohnen in Berlin. | The friends I write to live in Berlin. |
| **Dativ (f)** | Die Frau, **der** ich das Buch gebe, ist meine Mutter. | The woman I give the book to is my mother. |
| **Genitiv (m)** | Der Mann, **dessen** Auto rot ist, fährt schnell. | The man whose car is red drives fast. |
| **Genitiv (pl)** | Die Kinder, **deren** Eltern kommen, sind glücklich. | The children whose parents are coming are happy. |

💡 **Quick tip:** The hardest part of Relativsätze is determining the Case. Ask yourself: what's the role of the relative noun in the relative clause?
- Is it doing the action? → Nominativ
- Is the action being done to it? → Akkusativ
- After a preposition or Dativ verb? → Dativ
- Possession? → Genitiv
